\begin{abstract}
We reproduce the annulus Stokes benchmark of \citet{thieulot2018} (TP2018) using
Underworld3 (UW3). The benchmark describes incompressible isoviscous Stokes flow
in a cylindrical annulus with tangential velocity boundary conditions imposed on
both the inner and outer boundaries, driven by radially directed gravity and
analytically prescribed buoyancy forcing that generates convection cells
parameterised by the harmonic number \(k\). We investigate the accuracy and
convergence behaviour of several mixed finite-element discretisations, including
\(P_1\times P_0\), \(P_1\times P_1\), \(P_2\times P_0\),
\(P_2\times P_{-1}\), \(P_2\times P_1\), and \(P_3\times P_2\).
Numerical solutions are evaluated against the analytical
velocity and pressure fields using relative \(L_2\)-norm errors. The higher-order
Taylor--Hood discretisations reproduce the expected convergence hierarchy for
smooth Stokes solutions, with approximately third-order velocity and second-order
pressure convergence for \(P_2\times P_1\), and fourth-order velocity and
third-order pressure convergence for \(P_3\times P_2\). In contrast,
lower-order discretisations exhibit reduced robustness, particularly in pressure
recovery. These results demonstrate that UW3 accurately reproduces analytical
Stokes solutions in curved geometries and provides a robust framework for future
annulus geodynamics simulations.
\end{abstract}

\introduction

Accurate numerical solution of the incompressible Stokes equations is fundamental
to modelling mantle convection, lithospheric deformation, subduction dynamics,
and planetary evolution. Many geodynamic applications require simulations in
curved geometries, such as annulus, spherical cap, and full spherical shell.
These geometries introduce additional numerical challenges associated with
curved boundaries, pressure nullspaces, coordinate-dependent forcing terms, and
tangential boundary conditions.

Reproducing analytical benchmarks remains one of the most rigorous approaches
for assessing the correctness of numerical implementations. Unlike code-to-code
comparisons, analytical benchmarks enable direct quantification of
discretisation errors and convergence rates. They are particularly valuable for
geodynamic Stokes problems because they simultaneously assess
velocity--pressure coupling, incompressibility preservation, boundary condition
implementation, and the accurate representation of curved geometries
\citep{kramer2021,davies2022}.

The annulus benchmark introduced by TP2018 provides an exact analytical solution
for incompressible isoviscous Stokes flow in a cylindrical shell subjected to
radially directed gravity. The benchmark was specifically designed for
geodynamics applications and has since been widely used for verification of
geodynamic codes within the community \citep{gassmoller2024}. Unlike classical
Cartesian manufactured solutions, the benchmark incorporates curved inner and
outer boundaries, non-trivial pressure distributions along the boundaries,
coordinate-dependent buoyancy forcing, tangential velocity boundary conditions,
and controllable azimuthal complexity through the harmonic parameter \(k\).
These characteristics make the benchmark particularly well suited for assessing
finite-element formulations in annulus and curved-domain mantle convection
problems.

In this study, we reproduce the TP2018 annulus
benchmark to verify the curved-domain finite-element implementations in UW3. We
evaluate the convergence and accuracy of several mixed finite-element
discretisations, including both lower-order and higher-order
velocity--pressure element pairs. Numerical results are compared against the
analytical velocity and pressure fields using \(L_2\)-norm errors and
boundary pressure diagnostics. Particular attention is given to the convergence
behaviour of Taylor--Hood discretisations and the performance of lower-order and
equal-order element formulations.

\section{Governing Equations}

We consider steady state incompressible Stokes equations:
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
  -\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma} &= \rho \mathbf{g}, \\
  \nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} &= 0.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
The stress tensor is
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\sigma} = -p\mathbf{I} + 2\eta\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u}),
\end{equation}
with viscosity \(\eta\) and strain-rate tensor
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}(\mathbf{u})
  =
  \frac{1}{2}\left(\nabla \mathbf{u} + (\nabla \mathbf{u})^T\right).
\end{equation}
where \(\mathbf{u}\) is velocity, \(p\) is pressure, \(\rho\) is density, and \(\mathbf{g}\) is gravitational acceleration.

\section{Analytical Benchmark Solution}

Following \citet{thieulot2018}, the analytical velocity field in polar coordinates is defined as
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
  v_\theta(r,\theta) &= f(r)\cos(k\theta), \\
  v_r(r,\theta) &= k g(r)\sin(k\theta).
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
where
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
  f(r) &= Ar+\frac{B}{r}, \\
  g(r) &= \frac{A}{2}r+\frac{B}{r}\ln r+\frac{C}{r}.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
The constants \(A\) and \(B\) are chosen such that radial velocity vanishes at both annulus boundaries:
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
  A &= -C\frac{2(\ln R_1-\ln R_2)}{R_2^2\ln R_1-R_1^2\ln R_2}, \\
  B &= -C\frac{R_2^2-R_1^2}{R_2^2\ln R_1-R_1^2\ln R_2}.
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
The pressure field is
\begin{equation}
  p(r,\theta)=k h(r)\sin(k\theta)+\rho_0(R_2-r),
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
  h(r)=\frac{2g(r)-f(r)}{r}.
\end{equation}
The corresponding density field is
\begin{equation}
  \rho(r,\theta)=kM(r)\sin(k\theta)+\rho_0,
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
  M(r)=g''(r)-\frac{g'(r)}{r}
  -\frac{g(r)}{r^2}(k^2-1)
  +\frac{f(r)}{r^2}
  +\frac{f'(r)}{r}.
\end{equation}
The benchmark calculations use the standard parameter values
\[
  R_1=1,
  \qquad
  R_2=2,
  \qquad
  C=-1,
  \qquad
  \rho_0=0.
\]
where \(R_1\) is the inner radius and \(R_2\) is the outer radius.
Gravity acts radially inward:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{g} = -\mathbf{e}_r.
\end{equation}
The benchmark assumes constant viscosity \(\eta=1\) and prescribes analytical velocity on boundaries, and density field in the domain.
Increasing the harmonic parameter \(k\) increases azimuthal complexity and produces \(2k\) convection cells in the annulus.
This enables systematic evaluation of numerical performance across increasingly oscillatory flow structures.

\section{Numerical Methodology}

\subsection{UW3 Formulation}

The benchmark is implemented in Underworld3 (UW3), a next-generation
geodynamics framework built on PETSc and symbolic problem specification
in Python. The incompressible Stokes system is discretised using the
finite-element method and assembled through symbolic expressions defined
with SymPy. The resulting linear systems are solved using PETSc Krylov
subspace solvers and preconditioners.

To remove the pressure gauge ambiguity inherent to incompressible Stokes
flow, a constant-pressure nullspace is supplied to PETSc during the solve.
Following solution, the domain-average pressure is removed from the
numerical solution prior to error evaluation and comparison with the
analytical benchmark solution.

The annulus domain is discretised using unstructured triangular meshes
generated with progressively refined nominal cell sizes. All simulations
reported in this study employ linear geometric representation of the
curved annulus boundaries.

\subsection{Finite-Element Discretisations}

The following mixed finite-element pairs are evaluated:
\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Velocity space & Pressure space & Description \\
\midrule
\(P_1\) & \(P_0\) & Linear velocity, discontinuous constant pressure \\
\(P_1\) & \(P_1\) & Equal-order continuous discretisation \\
\(P_2\) & \(P_0\) & Quadratic velocity, discontinuous constant pressure \\
\(P_2\) & \(P_{-1}\) & Quadratic velocity, discontinuous linear pressure \\
\(P_2\) & \(P_1\) & Taylor--Hood discretisation \\
\(P_3\) & \(P_2\) & Higher-order Taylor--Hood discretisation \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The quadrature degree is chosen according to the highest polynomial order present in the discretisation.
For smooth incompressible Stokes solutions, stable Taylor--Hood-type discretisations are expected to exhibit velocity convergence of order \(p+1\) and pressure convergence of order \(p\) for a \(P_{p}\times P_{p-1}\) discretisation \citep{girault1986,boffi2013}.

\subsection{Error Metrics}

Errors are quantified by comparing the numerical and analytical
solutions using both absolute and relative \(L_2\)-norm measures.
The absolute \(L_2\)-norm error is defined as
\begin{equation}
  E_{L_2}(q)
  =
  \left(
  \int_\Omega |q-q^*|^2 \, d\Omega
  \right)^{1/2},
\end{equation}
and the relative \(L_2\)-norm error is computed as
\begin{equation}
  E^{\mathrm{rel}}_{L_2}(q)
  =
  \frac{E_{L_2}(q)}{\|q^*\|_{L_2}}
  =
  \left(
  \frac{\int_\Omega |q-q^*|^2\,d\Omega}
       {\int_\Omega |q^*|^2\,d\Omega}
  \right)^{1/2},
\end{equation}
where \(q\) and \(q^*\) denote the numerical and analytical
solutions, respectively.

Convergence rates are evaluated from successive mesh refinements as
\begin{equation}
  rate
  =
  \frac{
    \log\left(
      E_{L_2,h_1}(q) /
      E_{L_2,h_2}(q)
    \right)
  }
  {
    \log\left(h_1/h_2\right)
  },
\end{equation}
where \(h_1\) and \(h_2\) are the characteristic mesh sizes of two
consecutive refinements. For uniform mesh refinement with
\(h_2=h_1/2\), this reduces to
\begin{equation}
  rate
  =
  \log_2
  \left(
    \frac{E_{L_2,h_1}(q)}
         {E_{L_2,h_2}(q)}
  \right).
\end{equation}

\section{Results}

\subsection{Convergence Behaviour}

The convergence behaviour for all tested discretisations is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:thieulot_annulus_convergence}.
The \(P_2\times P_1\) Taylor--Hood discretisation exhibits approximately third-order velocity convergence and second-order pressure convergence, consistent with theoretical expectations for smooth Stokes solutions.

The higher-order \(P_3\times P_2\) discretisation achieves approximately fourth-order velocity convergence and third-order pressure convergence on coarse-to-intermediate mesh resolutions.
At the finest resolutions, convergence rates begin to plateau, likely reflecting accumulated effects from geometry approximation, quadrature errors, and solver tolerances.

The lower-order discretisations show reduced robustness.
The \(P_1\times P_0\) pair fails to produce reliable convergence behaviour for this benchmark, while the equal-order \(P_1\times P_1\) discretisation produces acceptable velocity convergence but weaker and less consistent pressure convergence.
The discontinuous-pressure \(P_2\times P_0\) pair gives approximately second-order velocity convergence and first-order pressure convergence, whereas \(P_2\times P_{-1}\) gives near second-order velocity convergence but poor pressure convergence over the available mesh refinements.
These observations are consistent with established finite-element theory for incompressible Stokes flow \citep{boffi2013,thieulot2025}.

Pressure profiles extracted along the inner and outer annulus boundaries are compared against analytical solutions in Fig.~\ref{fig:thieulot_annulus_boundary_pressure}.

\section{Discussion}

The present results demonstrate that Underworld3 reproduces the analytical annulus benchmark solution with convergence behaviour consistent with finite-element theory.
The benchmark additionally highlights several important numerical considerations.

The stability status of the lower-order and discontinuous-pressure pairs requires
some qualification. The equal-order \(P_1\times P_1\) pair is not inf-sup stable
as a standard Galerkin Stokes discretisation and is therefore susceptible to
spurious pressure modes unless an additional pressure-stabilisation mechanism is
introduced \citep{boffi2013}. Stabilised equal-order formulations, such as
Petrov--Galerkin, Galerkin least-squares, or related stabilised mixed methods, can
restore well-posedness and recover the smooth-solution target rates
\(E_{L_2}(\mathbf{u})/E_{L_2}(p)=2/1\) \citep{Hughes_1986,Brezzi_1988}.
The \(P_2\times P_0\) pair is inf-sup stable, but is non-standard and
approximation-unbalanced: the constant pressure space limits the expected
pressure convergence to first order and the velocity convergence to second order
\citep{boffi2013,thieulot2025}. The \(P_2\times P_{-1}\) pair used here denotes
continuous quadratic velocity with discontinuous linear pressure, i.e. the
lowest-order Scott--Vogelius-type \(P_2\times P_1^{\mathrm{disc}}\) pairing. This
pair is not uniformly stable on arbitrary triangular meshes, but can be made
stable on suitable split meshes, including barycentric/Alfeld refinements, where
the smooth-solution target is \(3/2\) \citep{Scott_1985,Guzm_n_2018}.

\subsection{Boundary Pressure Error Asymmetry}

The inner- and outer-boundary pressure errors in
Fig.~\ref{fig:thieulot_annulus_boundary_pressure_convergence} are not expected to
be equal, even though the analytical pressure has zero signed integral on each
boundary. With \(\rho_0=0\), the analytical boundary pressure is
\begin{equation}
  p^*(r,\theta) = k h(r)\sin(k\theta),
\end{equation}
and therefore
\begin{equation}
  \int_{\Gamma_r} p^*\,ds
  =
  \int_0^{2\pi} k h(r)\sin(k\theta)\,r\,d\theta
  =
  0.
\end{equation}
The error plotted in Fig.~\ref{fig:thieulot_annulus_boundary_pressure_convergence}
is instead an absolute boundary \(L_2\) error,
\begin{equation}
  E_{L_2}(p|_{\Gamma_r})
  =
  \left(
  \int_{\Gamma_r} |p_h-p^*|^2\,ds
  \right)^{1/2}
  =
  \left(
  \int_0^{2\pi} |p_h(r,\theta)-p^*(r,\theta)|^2 r\,d\theta
  \right)^{1/2}.
\end{equation}
The corresponding analytical pressure magnitude is
\begin{equation}
  \|p^*\|_{L_2(\Gamma_r)}
  =
  |k h(r)|\sqrt{\pi r},
\end{equation}
which is different on the two boundaries. For the present benchmark parameters,
\[
  h(R_1) \simeq 2.328,
  \qquad
  h(R_2) \simeq -0.918,
  \qquad
  \frac{\|p^*\|_{L_2(\Gamma_{R_2})}}
       {\|p^*\|_{L_2(\Gamma_{R_1})}}
  \simeq 0.558 .
\]
Thus the signed mean cancels on both boundaries, but the pressure trace has
larger \(L_2\) magnitude on the inner boundary.

A second source of asymmetry is the sensitivity of the pressure trace to small
geometric perturbations. If a discrete boundary point is displaced from the
exact circular boundary by
\[
  x_h(\theta)-x(\theta)
  =
  \delta r(\theta)\mathbf{e}_r
  +
  r\,\delta\theta(\theta)\mathbf{e}_\theta,
\]
then the analytical pressure sampled on the perturbed boundary satisfies, to
first order,
\begin{equation}
  p^*(r+\delta r,\theta+\delta\theta)-p^*(r,\theta)
  \simeq
  k h'(r)\sin(k\theta)\,\delta r
  +
  k^2 h(r)\cos(k\theta)\,\delta\theta .
\end{equation}
Consequently, a boundary-pressure error model contains the geometric component
\begin{equation}
  E_{L_2}^2(p|_{\Gamma_r})
  \approx
  r\int_0^{2\pi}
  \left|
    e_{\mathrm{disc}}(\theta)
    +
    k h'(r)\sin(k\theta)\,\delta r
    +
    k^2 h(r)\cos(k\theta)\,\delta\theta
  \right|^2 d\theta ,
\end{equation}
where \(e_{\mathrm{disc}}\) contains the pressure discretisation and solver
errors on the represented boundary. For the benchmark constants,
\[
  h'(R_1)\simeq -13.31,
  \qquad
  h'(R_2)\simeq -0.164 .
\]
The inner pressure trace is therefore far more sensitive to small radial
boundary-location errors than the outer trace. This explains why the absolute
inner-boundary error can be much larger than the outer-boundary error, despite
the outer boundary having a larger arclength measure.

Linear boundary elements approximate a circular boundary by straight chords. If
\(\Delta\) denotes the boundary mesh spacing, the geometric displacement is
typically \(O(\Delta^2)\), while the normal error is \(O(\Delta)\). Classical
curved-element and isoparametric finite-element estimates show how higher-order
geometry can recover the optimal approximation behaviour expected on smooth
domains \citep{Ciarlet_1972,Lenoir_1986,Bernardi_1989}. A curved isoparametric
element of geometric degree \(m\) uses
\begin{equation}
  x_h(\xi)=\sum_i N_i^{(m)}(\xi)X_i
\end{equation}
and improves these estimates to approximately
\begin{equation}
  \|x_h-x\|_{L^\infty(\Gamma)}=O(\Delta^{m+1}),
  \qquad
  \|n_h-n\|_{L^\infty(\Gamma)}=O(\Delta^m).
\end{equation}
This is particularly relevant for pressure because the normal stress contains
the pressure term
\begin{equation}
  \sigma(\mathbf{u},p)n
  =
  -p n + 2\eta \varepsilon(\mathbf{u})n .
\end{equation}
Replacing \(n\) by a discrete normal \(n_h\) perturbs the pressure contribution
by approximately \(-p(n_h-n)\). Higher-order curved geometry reduces this
normal and boundary-location error, and should therefore reduce boundary
pressure errors most strongly where \(|h'(r)|\) and \(|h(r)|\) are largest.

controlling the inner--outer error difference.

Boundary geometry order & Linear: \(O(\Delta^2)\) position, \(O(\Delta)\) normal; curved degree \(m\): \(O(\Delta^{m+1})\), \(O(\Delta^m)\) & Method-dependent & Curved elements reduce pressure-trace and stress-normal errors, especially on the inner boundary \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

First, higher-order mixed discretisations provide substantially improved pressure accuracy relative to lower-order formulations.
This is particularly important in geodynamics applications where pressure gradients strongly influence buoyancy-driven flow and dynamic topography.

Second, curved geometries remain sensitive to geometric approximation order.
Although linear geometry representation was sufficient to recover theoretical convergence trends for the present benchmark, higher-order curved geometry representations may become increasingly important for extremely fine meshes or strongly curved spherical-shell problems.

Third, equal-order discretisations continue to exhibit weaker pressure robustness relative to stable Taylor--Hood formulations.
This behaviour aligns with expectations from the Ladyzhenskaya--Babuska--Brezzi stability condition.

The benchmark therefore provides a valuable verification framework not only for Underworld3, but also for future development of curved-element geometries, advanced boundary-condition implementations, and spherical-shell finite-element formulations.

\conclusions

We have presented a verification study of the Thieulot--Puckett annulus Stokes benchmark using Underworld3.
The benchmark provides a stringent test of incompressible Stokes flow in curved geometries through the combination of radial gravity, coordinate-dependent forcing, tangential boundary velocity conditions, and non-trivial pressure variation on curved annulus boundaries.

The principal findings are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Higher-order Taylor--Hood discretisations reproduce the expected smooth-solution convergence hierarchy.
  \item The \(P_2\times P_1\) discretisation achieves approximately third-order velocity and second-order pressure convergence.
  \item The \(P_2\times P_0\) discretisation achieves approximately second-order velocity and first-order pressure convergence.
  \item The \(P_3\times P_2\) discretisation achieves approximately fourth-order velocity and third-order pressure convergence before fine-resolution error saturation.
  \item Lower-order discretisations exhibit weaker pressure robustness and reduced convergence quality.
  \item Underworld3 accurately reproduces analytical Stokes solutions in curved annular geometries.
\end{enumerate}

These results establish the Thieulot--Puckett annulus benchmark as an effective verification framework for Underworld3 annulus and spherical-shell geodynamics applications.
